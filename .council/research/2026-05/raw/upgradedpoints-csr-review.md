## Premium-tier deltas (CSR vs CSP)

What changes between mid-tier and premium-tier reviews on UP:

1. **More H3 benefit sub-sections** — CSR review has ~12 sub-benefits (Annual Credits, Airport Lounge Access, Global Entry credit, The Edit by Chase, IHG One Platinum Elite, Primary Auto Rental Coverage, Trip Interruption, Trip Delay, Baggage Delay, Purchase Protection, Extended Warranty, Roadside Assistance) vs CSP's ~6.
2. **A "Perks Unlocked With Annual Spending" H2** appears on premium cards that have spend-tiered benefits — not on mid-tier.
3. **Higher dollar values inline** — "$2,000 in annual credits", "$10,000 maximum coverage", "$3,000 per person per trip" — the premium card's review is denser in specific numbers.
4. **More comparison alternatives** in the `Alternative Cards` H2 — premium cards face more cross-shopping.
5. **Same rating** (`4.59`) — UP doesn't grade premium cards higher just because they cost more.

## "Great Card If / Don't Get If" — premium tier wording

> ## Great Card If
> - You're a frequent traveler who wants a card with premium travel, dining, and lifestyle perks.
> - You can easily take advantage of the various annual credits for general travel, hotels, dining, StubHub, Lyft, and DoorDash.
> - You want a card with Chase Sapphire Lounge and Priority Pass airport lounge access.
> - You want elite benefit treatment during your hotel stays via The Edit by Chase hotel collection and complimentary IHG One Rewards Platinum Elite status.
> - You're looking for a card that provides top-notch travel insurance and purchase protection.
>
> ## Don't Get If
> - You don't travel much.
> - You prefer a card with a lower annual fee.

Premium-card "Don't Get If" is shorter than mid-tier (CSP had 3 bullets; CSR has 2). Implication: premium cards have a narrower self-disqualification frame — if you travel and can afford the fee, the card works; if you don't, it doesn't.
